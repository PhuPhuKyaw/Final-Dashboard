Academic Journey Dashboard

A self-reflective data analysis mapping study habits, grades, mental stress, and lifestyle boundaries over one academic year.

📖 Story Overview

What is this story? This dashboard visualizes a single student's personal academic journey across two semesters (Fall and Spring), tracking how variables like weekly study hours, sleep, screen time, and stress levels correlate directly with final academic letter grades.

Why is it meaningful? Modern students face an optimization problem: balancing academic excellence with personal well-being. By tracking these metrics weekly, this data moves past grade points and trends to expose the student's strength, weakness and the real cost of a high GPA—showing exactly where stress spikes, when sleep is sacrificed, and how social media serves as either an escape or a distraction.

🔍 Key Insights

  • Study hours vs Stress Level correlation: A clear negative correlation exists between Study_Hours_Per_Week and Stress_Level. This means that as your weekly study hours increase, your reported stress levels drastically decrease, or vice versa.
  • Study hours and Subject performance: Study hours are slightly balance among the subjects. There is no correlation between Grade and Study hours for all departments. Even though Seminar 2 has the highest study hour, its grade is lower than less study hour subjects such as Statistics, Probability and Macroeconomics. However, there are moderately positive correlation between study hour and grade for each department.
  • Study Hour Pattern: There are recurring wave pattern in the study habbit. The repeating peaks occur 4 times around exam weeks: midterms and finals. The sudden spikes points out my procrastination habit. This explains why the hours are so high but the grades remain lower in Pillar courses.
  • Pillar vs Mathematics courses Pillar courses have the highest average study hour during exam weeks. Compared to fall semester, there is a flatter line for pillar department during spring semester. This indicates a steady workload (like weekly readings or writing) rather than sudden exam study. Although Mathematics and sciences courses have the lowest average study hour, they are the highest performing courses. This supports that I have a stronger foundational background in mathematics and sciences than pillar courses.
  • Screen time Reduction Period: My screen time drops sharply drop by about 8 hours but study time increases during exam weeks.

Data-Driven Decisions

Based on my academic data,

  • Study habit and Time allocation: I should study more consistently, even immediately after exams, so that I will not have a heavy workload or stressful periods during exam weeks. I should also spend more time and avoid procrastinating, especially in Pillar and social science courses, because they require qualitative analysis, and failing to do so often leads to low-quality papers.
  • Study method: I should also change my study method such as doing weekly reading and writing regularly, practicing critical thinking.
  • Screen Time reduction: I need to reduce my screen time, even during regular weeks. This would help me increase my study hours and maintain more consistent study habits.

⚖️ Ethics & Responsibility Section

🔒 Privacy Statement

Data Included: This dataset captures personal academic logs, weekly self-reported metrics (sleep, stress levels), and basic institutional metadata (course departments). The data includes only Mathematics courses, and no programming courses.

Anonymization: Instructor details and course sections have been entirely excluded to preserve student and institutional anonymity.

⚠️ Bias & Limitation Disclosure

Memory Bias & Subjectivity: Metrics like Stress Level (1-10) and weekly Study Hours, Sleep hour and Screen time are self-reported. So, they may contain mistakes or bias from recent emotions and experiences.

Dataset Scale: The dataset tracks only one student during a single academic period. Hence, findings cannot be generalized to broader student populations.

📊 Visualization Justification

Chart Choices:

  • A Scatter plot was utilized to see the clear correlation between Study hour and Stress level.
  • A bar chart was used to compare numerical values (Study hour) across distinct categories (Subject).
  • A scatter plot was used again to show there is a moderate correlation between Study hour and Academic performance in each department.
  • A line chart explicitly shows pattern changes over a semester timeline, helping to identify trends, fluctuations, and relationships between my behavior and performance.

Misinterpretation Risk: Correlation does not mean causation. For example, even if study hours and stress levels have a negative correlation, it does not prove that studying more directly reduces stress. Factors such as exam and subject difficulty, sleep hour, and personal circumstances might influence stress level.

🎯 Responsible Decision Limits

  • Most of my decisions promote constant action and discipline. For instance, I assume more studying = less stress. But this could be reversed. When stress levels are low, it might be easier to sit down and focus for long hours. So, students should understand the importance of healthy relaxtion.
  • Since the data does not include programming courses, which need a lot of practice, conclusions based only on math courses may underestimate the workload and study demands of programming courses.
